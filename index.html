Project Overview

Context

The FIFA World Cup is one of the most watched sporting event on the planet — and the referees who officiate it carry enormous influence over its outcomes. FIFA assigns referees carefully, explicitly attempting to avoid assigning officials to matches involving teams from their own confederation. But does this policy work in practice?

Research Questions

This project investigates whether World Cup referees issue fewer yellow cards to teams from their own confederation — a measurable proxy for unconscious or conscious bias in officiating decisions. Using match and booking data from 1970 to 2022 across 13 tournaments and 764 matches, we ask: do same-confederation matchups result in systematically lower card counts for the home confederation's teams?

Methodology

This project uses the number of yellow cards issued per team per match as the primary measure of referee bias. Yellow cards are chosen because they represent a broad and more discretionary range of disciplinary decisions (meanwhile, red cards are relatively rare and often issued for clear violent conduct, which leaves less room for subjective judgment).

Referee bias is explored through four layers of data visualizations, from looking at the overall picture to investigating bias at the granular match level:

  • Layer 1 - Confederation Bias Matrix: a heatmap showing mean yellow cards issued across all referee-team confederation pairings
  • Layer 2 - Compare Card Distribution: distribution of yellow cards for same-confederation vs. cross-confederation matchups, supported by statistical test
  • Layer 3 - Bias Over Time: bias index tracked across all tournaments since 1970, with a rolling average to reveal the pattern over five decades
  • Layer 4 - High Stakes Spotlight: individual match card difference by tournament stage, examining if referee bias persists under the highest pressure.

Statistical Test - Mann-Whitney U Hypothesis Testing

The difference in yellow card distribution showned by the violin plots is subtle, so we added a statistical element to verify if there is a statistically significant difference between the two groups of team-referee matchups. We decided to use the Mann-Whitney U Test rather than a t-test because yellow card counts are discrete integers that do not follow a normal distribution — making a non-parametric test the more appropriate choice.

Hypotheses

- H₀: Same-confederation teams receive yellow cards at the same rate as cross-confederation teams (any observed difference is due to chance).
- H₁: Same-confederation teams receive fewer yellow cards than cross-confederation teams — consistent with referee bias.

Test Results

At a significance level of α = 0.05, the test yields

  • U = 203,682
  • p = 0.000488 (one-tailed)

The p-value is well below the threshold. Therefore, we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is evidence that the difference in yellow cards in two groups - same- and different-confederation team-referee matchup - is statistically significant.

This result supports the visual pattern observed earlier, motivating a deeper investigation into whether this effect persists over time and under different match conditions.

Findings

Overall Findings

Visualizations and statistical tests suggest that, across 13 FIFA World Cup tournaments from 1970 and 2022, same-confederation referee assignments are associated with fewer yellow cards issued to the favored team, and this pattern persists across different confederation pairings. Bias fluctuated over time, and it tends to weaken at the highest stakes matches

Layer 1 - Confederation Bias Matrix

  • Diagonal cells (same-confederation matchup) consistently show lower mean yellow card counts than off-diagonal cells in the same row, which suggests that referees tend to give teams from their confederation fewer yellow cards
  • UEFA diagonal (1.518) is noticeably among the lowest values in its row (and in the whole matrix) despite UEFA referee - UEFA team dominating referee appointments

Layer 2 - Compare Card Distribution

  • Same-confederation matchups produce a median of 1 yellow card per team per match, compared to 2 for cross-confederation matchups
  • The difference is statistically significant (U = 203,682, p < 0.001, one-tailed, α = 0.05). The violin distributions are visually subtle but the statistical test confirms the signal is real

Layer 3 - Bias Over Time

  • The bias index fluctuates considerably across tournaments, indicating that confederation bias is not a stable, consistent phenomenon but varies significantly year to year
  • At the individual match level, the bias signal is highly variable — individual matches can produce card differentials ranging from -6 to +5, reflecting the inherent noise in single-match officiating decisions. The systematic bias only becomes visible in aggregate, which explains why the rolling average is essential for interpreting the year-by-year fluctuations
  • Post-2018 VAR introduction, the bias index settles close to zero — consistent with the hypothesis that technology reduces discretionary officiating decisions and limits the opportunity for unconscious bias

Layer 4 - High-Stakes Spotlight

  • Same-confederation teams consistently receive fewer cards across Group Stage, Round of 16, Quarter-finals, and Semi-finals (though subtly visible) — red dots dominate the negative half at every stage, and mean dots sit below zero. The bias signal was the strongest in semi-finals.
  • The Final reverses the pattern, as the mean dot flips above zero (+0.5), meaning same-confederation teams actually received more cards in Finals. This may reflect FIFA's stricter referee selection for the most scrutinized match, or simply the small sample size (few Finals with same-confederation alignment)
  • Individual match variance is high, as card differences range from -5 to +4 across all stages, confirming the bias signal is subtle and only detectable in aggregate, not at the individual match level
